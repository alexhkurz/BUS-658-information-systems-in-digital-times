Week 9 Question: Jordan Ehrman

If digital platforms already function as governors of social and economic life, should the main goal be to make them more competitive, more democratic, or more prosocial? Can those goals actually coexist, and do we see them coexisting in other spaces? 

I keep wondering whether competition policy alone can ever fix systems whose harms come not just from monopoly, but from the deeper fact that platforms and AI systems organize human perception, coordination, and legitimacy in the first place. Put a little more dramatically: even if we break the giants up, do we still end up living inside machines that govern us unless we redesign the social logic of the platforms themselves?

My conversation with 5.4 found that all of the proposed changes are necessary but not individually conducive to a solution. A fragmented media landscape is not better if they're individually more driven to extract value from their platforms in order to stay competitive, and such a market force can poison the gamut of platforms uniformly. 

But, these problems have many parallels throughout history that come with solutions to be learned from. These solutions are often active, and therefore temporary. 

AI Response from 5.4: 

 

Yeah — **kind of, but never cleanly**. The rough historical pattern is that when a communication or coordination system becomes socially indispensable *and* structurally prone to monopoly or manipulation, societies usually do one of three things: they treat it like a **utility/common carrier**, they impose **public-interest obligations**, or they build **non-market civic institutions** around it. Railroads and later telephone systems are classic “you cannot let this be pure private whim” cases: because they tended toward monopoly, they got regulated to require broad service, nondiscrimination, safety, and “just and reasonable” rates rather than being left as totally freewheeling winner-take-all markets. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

Broadcasting is probably the closest pre-digital analogy to today’s platform problem, because society more or less said: *you are not merely a business; you are using scarce public infrastructure and shaping public discourse, so you owe the public something back.* In the U.S., that showed up in the FCC’s “public interest” framework and, for a long stretch, the Fairness Doctrine, which required licensed broadcasters to cover controversial public issues and present contrasting views. But that was only a **partial containment**, not a perfect solution: it rested on spectrum scarcity, it raised real free-speech concerns, and critics argued it could be politically abused or chill speech. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][2])

There’s also a different family of analogs that looks less like regulation and more like **social design**: libraries, parks, unions, churches, clubs, fraternal societies, mutual-aid groups, neighborhood associations — spaces that created norms, belonging, trust, and reciprocity without optimizing every interaction for extraction. Historically, fraternal organizations in the U.S. were massive and provided real mutual support at scale; by 1920, one estimate put membership at roughly one in three adult men, and these organizations often functioned as reciprocal welfare systems rather than pure status clubs. That does not map perfectly onto social media, obviously, but it shows that societies *have* built infrastructures whose “business model,” so to speak, was social cohesion rather than attention capture. ([Social Welfare History Project][3])

So my spicy answer is: **yes, we have seen pieces of this solved, but mostly by refusing to let every important sphere operate under the logic of maximum extraction**. We solved parts of it in transport and telecom by saying “this must be broadly accessible and not arbitrarily discriminatory.” We solved parts of it in broadcasting by saying “if you shape the public sphere, you owe public obligations.” And we solved parts of it in civic life by building institutions whose point was not growth, virality, or monetization at all. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

But — and this is the nasty little goblin in the wiring — **none of those solutions fully solved the underlying human problem**. Railroads still had robber barons. Broadcasting still had propaganda, exclusion, and capture. Civic organizations often built real solidarity *for insiders* while excluding outsiders by race, gender, religion, or class. So the lesson is not “ah yes, history solved this already”; it is more like: **history shows that incentive structures can be tamed, but only through a mix of rules, institution-building, and norms, and every one of those can calcify or get captured.** ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

Honestly, I think the pre-digital world’s biggest advantage was that it had more **friction and pluralism of institutions**. Your church was not your newspaper was not your workplace was not your town square was not your dating market was not your shopping mall. Now one platform can be six things at once, which makes failures more total and governance much harder. That’s why the “just make digital markets competitive” answer feels incomplete to me: if a platform is simultaneously infrastructure, media, social venue, and identity machine, then competition matters, but **constitutional design for the medium** matters too.

My own take is that the best analog is probably a **hybrid** of old telecom/common-carrier logic and old civic-institution logic. In other words: some layers should be treated like infrastructure and be interoperable, nondiscriminatory, and portable; other layers should be designed like communities with actual norms, deliberation, and user agency. The mistake was letting the entire thing become one giant casino that also happens to raise your children and tell you what reality is 😌
